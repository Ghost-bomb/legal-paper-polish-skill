# Academic Expression Reference

Expression references are not final drafts. They are local models for how the author's confirmed intention can be expressed more naturally and academically.

## Default Scope

- Output expression references for 1-3 natural paragraphs by default.
- If the user asks for a long section or whole article, first complete segmentation and plain-language diagnosis.
- Do not provide a full section rewrite unless the user has confirmed paragraph intentions and scope.

## Two Versions

When useful, provide:

- 稳妥贴近原文版: preserve structure, claims, and wording as much as possible while removing stiffness and clarifying logic.
- 自然推进增强版: adjust sentence order and transitions to better follow reader doubts, while preserving framework, views, and support.

## Core Style Rules

- Let the sentence answer a reader question.
- Make the legal task visible: characterization, rule application, exception, proof, boundary, consequence, or response.
- Keep judgment strength accurate: possible, likely, generally, under specific conditions, or only in some cases.
- Prefer concrete legal relations over broad abstract nouns.
- Use transitions only when they mark a real reasoning move.
- Make material entry purposeful: "this material matters because..."

## Avoid

- Adding "问题在于、进一步而言、由此可见、在这一意义上" as automatic decoration.
- Turning all sentences into firm judgment sentences.
- Mechanically using "首先、其次、再次、最后" when points are not sequential.
- Writing policy-report language that lists tasks without analysis.
- Making a simple point look mature by adding abstract nouns.
- Importing new cases, norms, literature, or substantive views.

## Rewrite Procedure

1. Restate the confirmed plain-language intent in one sentence.
2. Identify what the original fails to make readable.
3. Preserve footnote-sensitive propositions and authority attribution.
4. Improve sequence: reader doubt -> response -> material -> analysis -> boundary -> next step.
5. Provide two versions only if they offer a real tradeoff.
6. Add risk notes for any sentence that depends on footnotes, quotes, cases, or legal authority.

## Signs Of A Good Reference

- The reader can see why the paragraph starts where it starts.
- Materials no longer feel dumped into the paragraph.
- The paragraph does not overclaim.
- The ending creates a real next step rather than a ceremonial summary.
- The author's own intention remains visible.
