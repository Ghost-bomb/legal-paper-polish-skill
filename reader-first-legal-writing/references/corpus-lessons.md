# Corpus Lessons

These rules abstract writing movement from 20 Chinese legal journal Markdown articles. Do not reuse corpus views, cases, authorities, footnotes, materials, or distinctive wording.

## Mature Openings

- Start from a concrete institutional change, recurring dispute, adjudicative difficulty, regulatory pressure, or conceptual mismatch.
- Do not begin with a broad slogan unless it immediately produces a legal question.
- Move in this order when possible: background -> tension -> legal problem -> research object -> why existing discussion is insufficient -> route.
- A useful opening lets the reader feel: "I see why this is a legal problem now."

## From Phenomenon To Legal Question

- Convert social or policy facts into legal problems by naming the affected legal relation, rule, procedure, standard, remedy, or institutional competence.
- Avoid jumping from "this phenomenon is important" to "therefore this article studies it."
- Ask what the phenomenon does to an existing legal category: expands it, exposes a gap, creates conflict, weakens enforcement, shifts risk, or changes incentives.

## Limiting The Object

- State what is being studied and what is not being studied before the argument becomes too large.
- Use limitation to reduce reader anxiety, not to decorate the introduction.
- Good limitations explain why the chosen object is the decisive pressure point.

## Setting Problem Groups

- Prefer question clusters that follow a legal sequence: characterization -> applicable rule -> conflict or deficiency -> consequence -> response.
- Avoid listing three parallel questions that do not depend on each other.
- Each later question should feel necessary because the previous answer created or exposed it.

## Section Openings

- Do not mechanically restate the heading.
- Begin by answering: "After the last section, what would a reader naturally ask?"
- Common section-opening functions:
  - explain why a concept must be clarified before applying it;
  - show why a practical scene must be classified before doctrine enters;
  - identify the limit of a previous path;
  - move from descriptive facts to normative evaluation;
  - narrow a broad conflict into a specific clause, element, or remedy.

## Paragraph Rhythm

Use this rhythm as a diagnostic lens, not as a rigid template:

1. Reader doubt: what question would arise here?
2. Author response: what judgment answers it?
3. Material entry: what source, rule, case, literature, or fact is needed?
4. Analysis: how does the material support, limit, or complicate the judgment?
5. Boundary: what should not be overclaimed?
6. Transition: what remains for the next paragraph or section?

## Turning Materials Into Steps

- Before presenting a case, norm, statistic, or literature view, explain why that material matters.
- After presenting it, say what it proves and what it does not prove.
- Use materials to perform specific tasks: identify a scene, confirm a rule structure, expose a doctrinal gap, distinguish alternatives, or support a limitation.
- Do not let footnotes carry logic that the main text has not stated.

## Natural And Steady Legal Tone

- Mature tone comes from controlled judgment strength, not from more abstract nouns.
- Prefer sentences that explain why a conclusion follows over sentences that only announce the conclusion.
- Use transitions to mark reasoning movement: contrast, limitation, consequence, precondition, exception, or shift of level.
- A paragraph sounds natural when a reader can reconstruct the author's thought without guessing missing steps.

## Avoid Empty Elevation

- Do not inflate every local issue into "system reconstruction," "governance modernization," or "paradigm transformation."
- Use broad values only after the concrete legal mechanism has been shown.
- If a paragraph contains many high-level nouns but no rule, element, actor, condition, or consequence, it likely needs grounding.

## Anti-AI Signals

- Repeatedly restating the thesis without new analytical work.
- Stacking connectors such as "首先、其次、再次、最后" where the points are not logically sequenced.
- Using "问题在于、进一步而言、由此可见、在这一意义上" as filler.
- Writing all sentences as firm judgments without showing hesitation, boundary, evidence, or route.
- Adding decorative bridge sentences that do not answer a reader question.
- Treating policy background, literature, and cases as separate piles rather than steps in one argument.
