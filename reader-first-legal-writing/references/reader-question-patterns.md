# Reader Question Patterns

Use these questions to guide diagnosis and coaching. For each paragraph, identify which reader doubt it should answer.

## Why does this phenomenon become a legal problem?

Guide the user to name the affected legal relation, doctrine, clause, procedure, remedy, competence, or standard. If the answer remains "because it is important," ask what legal consequence follows.

## Why is the research object limited here?

Ask what would become unmanageable or misleading without the limitation. A good answer explains why this object is the decisive point, not merely a convenient sample.

## Why does this issue need the next section?

Ask what the previous section has not yet resolved. The next section should answer a residue, not repeat the heading.

## What does this case, norm, literature, or fact prove?

Ask the user to complete: "I include this material because it shows ____." If the blank cannot be filled, the material is probably ornamental or misplaced.

## Why is this path only theoretically possible?

Ask what institutional condition, textual limitation, evidentiary burden, procedure, or policy constraint prevents the path from working fully.

## Is this conclusion too strong?

Check whether the paragraph has turned "may," "in some cases," "under this condition," or "some scholars argue" into an unconditional claim. Ask which conditions must remain.

## Why can the next judgment not follow directly?

Ask the user to identify the missing bridge: definition, classification, normative premise, empirical premise, exception, or comparison.

## Is this about claim construction or liability establishment?

Ask what legal task is being performed. If the paragraph mixes construction, violation, causation, remedy, and policy response, split or reorder it.

## Is this a factual effect or a normative evaluation?

Ask whether the sentence describes what happens in practice or judges whether it is legally acceptable. If both appear together, require an explicit bridge.

## Is this material explanation or the author's own judgment?

Ask the user to mark which sentences belong to sources and which are the author's inference. This protects attribution and helps readers know when the author is speaking.

## Is the paragraph answering, excluding, or preparing?

Classify the paragraph as:

- answering a reader doubt;
- excluding a misunderstanding or alternative path;
- preparing a concept, fact pattern, or rule for later analysis.

If none fits, ask the user why the paragraph is needed.

## Has the reader been told where to look?

Ask what the paragraph wants the reader to notice in a long list, case, or rule. Add a purpose sentence before material if needed.
